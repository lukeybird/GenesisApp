The United Kingdom Begins

The Anointing at Hebron

In 2 Samuel 5:1-5, we witness the fulfillment of God's promise to David. After Saul's death, all the tribes of Israel come to David at Hebron and anoint him king over all Israel. This marks the beginning of the united kingdom and points forward to Christ, the ultimate King who unites all believers.

All the tribes of Israel came to David at Hebron and said, "We are your own flesh and blood. In the past, while Saul was king over us, you were the one who led Israel on their military campaigns. And the Lord said to you, 'You will shepherd my people Israel, and you will become their ruler.'" 2 Samuel 5:1-2

David's anointing as king over all Israel fulfills God's promise made to him years earlier. The tribes recognize David as the one chosen by God to lead them. This moment represents the unification of the nation under one king, pointing forward to how Christ unites all believers into one kingdom.

Additional References
1 Samuel 16:13
David's original anointing by Samuel, showing God's choice of David as king.
Revelation 11:15
The kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Messiah, and he will reign for ever and ever, showing that Christ is the ultimate King.

Jerusalem: The City of David

David establishes Jerusalem as his capital, making it the center of the united kingdom. This city becomes the place where God's presence dwells and points forward to the New Jerusalem, where Christ reigns forever.

David's capture of Jerusalem and establishment of it as his capital is significant. This city becomes known as the City of David and later becomes the site of the temple. Jerusalem represents God's presence among His people and points forward to the New Jerusalem, where Christ reigns as the eternal King.

Christ Our Eternal King

Just as David united the tribes of Israel and established Jerusalem as his capital, Christ unites all believers and establishes His eternal kingdom. David's reign in Jerusalem points forward to Christ's eternal reign in the New Jerusalem.

Conclusion: The King Who Unites
